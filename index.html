NoiseMap NYC

Renters typically evaluate neighbourhoods based on a single visit, reflecting only current conditions failing to reflect conditions over their tenancy period. Prolonged noise exposure exceeding the WHO threshold of 55 decibels is linked to chronic health conditions including cardiovascular disease and sleep disorders. Moreover, research also indicates that noise above this threshold can reduce apartment rental values by approximately 2% per unit of noise increase, making this both a health and financial concern. However, no existing tools forecast how the noise level in a neighborhood can change over the year, nor a breakdown of the specific sources. This project addresses this gap by utilizing publicly available data to predict future neighbourhood noise levels, attribute them to their dominant sources, and deliver findings through an interactive map

The system ingests five open data sources: NYC construction permit filings, Traffic volume count, MapPLUTO zoning data, NYC 311 noise complaints and acoustic readings from SONYC urban sensor network. The data are aligned to a standardised 200x200 metre grid, resulting in a unified matrix used to train the XGBoost regression model for monthly noise prediction generation over a 12 months timeline, incorporating approved future permits and seasonal traffic projection as forward looking inputs. SHAP is then applied to decompose each prediction into the contribution of each noise source and displayed to users as human readable badges such as construction and traffic.
